1.1 Project Background

Botswana’s tourism landscape has been based on wildlife, particularly the ‘Big Five’, and concentrated in the Ngamiland and Chobe districts. This has resulted in uneven geographical distribution of tourism related infrastructure. Meanwhile, other parts of the country with limited populations and diversity of wildlife have remained unexplored, despite being well endowed with rich cultural and traditional practices. Manyana village has good potential for eco-tourism. Besides its attractive hilly landscape the village has geographical and cultural features and sites of historical importance. The potential offered by these features to eco-tourism has, however, not been exploited. Important historical features if left undeveloped could end up being destroyed or even displaced by other developments.

1.1.1 Objectives

The objectives of the study were to:

  • assess the potential and viability of community-based tourism focusing on cultural and historical assets in Manyana;
  • ensure effective participation of the community of Manyana in the design and execution of the various tourism ventures;
  • generate information on conservation threats faced by the cultural assets in Manyana, thereby creating an incentive for sustainable use of natural resources.

1.2 Project Area Description

Manyana is in the Southern district (Moshupa sub-district) about 17 kilometres east of Moshupa and approximately 50km from the District Administration at Kanye. Manyana has good tar road linkages with Gaborone and the neighbouring Mankgodi village. The Manyana area extends beyond the built up area of the village. Localities of Manyana include Bikwe, Boswelakgosi, Thakadiawa, Fikeng, Rathogwana, Makokwe, Bikwane, Dialane, Manyelanong, Ramating, Mokata, and Sekgweng.

1.2.1.1 Ecotourism

Ecotourism has been defined as a form of nature based tourism, with a set of principles and as a specific tourism market niche. Our understanding of ecotourism throughout this report is based on the IUCN definition and on the principles described by the Botswana Ecotourism Strategy of 2002. According to the IUCN, and the Botswana Ecotourism Strategy of 2002 ecotourism is environmentally responsible travel to areas of natural and cultural heritage to sustainably utilise natural resources to economically benefit the local communities and the environment in which they are found, to preserve the local cultures, traditions and history, to preserve cultural integrity and to increase level of awareness and tolerance on both the tourist and the receiving communities.

1.2.1.2 Community Based Tourism

According to World Wildlife Fund International community based tourism is a form of ecotourism where local communities have substantial control over the tourism initiatives. In this approach the communities have control over tourism developments and activities within their area and are often actively involved in the development and management of the tourism operations and a major proportion of the benefits remain in the community. The main objectives of community based tourism are:

  • To improve the economic status of the community members;
  • Inclusion of the community in participatory decision making
  • Provision of an alternative economy;

  • Exchanging knowledge, awareness and understanding of the indigenous culture.
How the community is defined depends on social and institutional structures in place. In this instance the Manyana community refers to the people of Manyana individually and collectively, or anyone settled in Manyana and who associates himself/ herself with the people and the local tribal authorities. This definition acknowledges the complexity and heterogeneity of local communities and the effect that this may have on the intrinsic and perceived worth of the sites by the members of the community. Exploitation of cultural heritage will not only reduce over-emphasis on wildlife based tourism but also diversify the economy and improve livelihoods of the local people. This would create an incentive for sustainable use of natural resources by the community.
