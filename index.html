About The Spectacular Project

Details Of Project Proponent

The community of Manyana represented by the Manyana Community Development Trust (MACODET), and in partnership with Thusano Lefatsheng (TL), wants to explore opportunities for community based tourism developments in their village

These developments will be based on the rich cultural, historic and natural resources of the village. This report is an evaluation of the tourism, archaeological, land use, natural resource and social situation in Manyana. It describes and interprets the current state of the tourism environment in the village and how available resources may be converted into a tourism product for the community. Opportunities and constraints posed by the natural and social environments are described to identify viable options for community based tourism in the village.

Authority

The MACODET Notarial Deed of Trust confers powers on the Trust as the supreme body that will oversee the utilisation of both the natural and cultural heritage for the economic development of the village. However, currently MACODET does not have any control over the cultural heritage (sites) that the community proposes to use for tourism development. The sites are under the control of the National Museum, Monuments and Art Gallery. The Village Development Committee is seen at the same level with MACODET as it is responsible for the disbursement of monies accrued through MACODET to village sub-committees. The checks and balances of the relationships between MACODET, VDC and the community is the responsibility of the Bogosi (Traditional Authority). The responsibilities of the above structures could therefore be summarised thus:

Responsibilities

• MACODET: Natural Resources and cultural heritage utilisation


It is therefore clear that MACODET will be the leading structure for guiding the proposed community based tourism. Other structures such as the Youth (Motlhware) Group and the Village Conservation Committee could be brought under MACODET as Sub-Committees.

Archaeological Background

The community based ecotourism

Manyana Rock Paintings

Manyana rock paintings are located on a cliff west of the village. The rock art, found on panels and numbering up to 55, occur in five separate areas that cover approximately 750 meters of cliff face. Some are found in shelters. The paintings include giraffe, antelope, human figure, ‘crocodile figures’ and geometric designs. Many of these are now faint, while others are almost indistinguishable.

The state of conservation of the rock art is poor. The paintings have faded and in some cases vandalized. If no immediate intervention is undertaken the rock art is likely to disappear. This would reduce the aesthetic and tourism value of the site. However, through the development of a conservation plan the remaining paintings can be protected to some extent from both human and natural threats. The National Museum controls access, has hired staff, fenced the area and put up sign boards at the site.

Livingstone’s Tree

David Livingstone’s tree is a national monument gazetted in 1938. It is probably a large fig (motlhatsa, Ficus salicifolia) tree located southwest of the Manyana village. A recent visit showed the tree to be quite healthy even though that may be because of the rainy season. Some of the branches touch the ground and may be rooted. The tree is attractive to tourists and bears historic and aesthetic beauty. To maintain proper Prefeasibility Study for Manyana Community Based Ecotourism Project June 2009 Prepared by Ecosurv Page 12 growth of the tree, pruning is needed. The tree is fenced and therefore protected. The site is available through guided tours.

Dimawe and Boswelakgosi

The Dimawe area is located in the middle of the fields with no clear open route and that naturally makes it less accessible compared to the Manyana rock paintings site. Dimawe consists of a group of three granite hills, two in the east and a smaller one in the west. The largest hill is known as Boswelakgosi, ‘The place where the kgosi died’. This is where the great grandfather of Kgosi Mareko Mosielele, the current Bahurutshe kgosi in Manyana was buried. However, the location of the grave is uncertain. Dimawe was only gazetted as a National Monument in 2006. The lower part of Dimawe hills (in the west) has extensive stonewalls associated with the Bangwaketse.

Dimawe is one of the most important historic places in Botswana (Ramsay 1991). It was a battle-field where the Batswana defeated the Boers in 1852 under the leadership of Kgosi Sechele of Bakwena. The Dimawe hills have symbolic value because Bakwena warriors defended their land in battlefield from Boer imperialism in 1852. Kgosi Sechele’s headquarters were based at Dimawe. He had constructed stone-wall fortifications for defence purposes against possible attack by the Boers, who in 1844 demanded that he surrender his guns and submit to their authority (Ramsay 1991).
